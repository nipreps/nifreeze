# Eddy-current-induced distortion

The distortion specific to diffusion MRI: the strong diffusion-encoding
gradients induce **eddy currents** in the scanner's conducting structures, which
produce a residual off-resonance field during readout and hence a geometric
distortion that changes with every gradient direction. Notation follows
[[andersson-2016-integrated-eddy]].

## Parameterization of the eddy field

The eddy-current-induced off-resonance field is written $e(\beta)$ (in Hz),
parameterised by a vector $\beta$ — "four for linear, ten for quadratic, etc."
The field is modelled as a spatial polynomial in the voxel coordinates whose
coefficients are $\beta$. Unlike the susceptibility field, $e(\beta)$ is
generated by the gradients and is therefore **fixed in scanner space**, not in
the subject — the asymmetry that motivates handling the two fields separately
([[concept-epi-off-resonance-distortion]]).

Its effect is a PE-direction displacement $d(e(\beta), a)$, exactly as for any
off-resonance field ([[concept-epi-off-resonance-distortion]], Eq. A3).

## Linear model is insufficient; use a higher-order model

A key empirical result: the **linear** eddy-current model commonly used (e.g. by
the older `eddy_correct`) is inadequate for high-resolution, high-b data; a
**quadratic** (second-order) spatial model performs significantly better. The
two are distinguished at the *second level* by the design matrix relating the
per-direction eddy coefficients to the gradient vector.

## Second-level model: regularizing across directions

Rather than estimate each volume's $\beta_i$ independently, the framework relates
them to the gradient direction $\mathbf{g}_i$ that produced them (Appendix). For
volume $i$ at second-level iteration $j$,

$$ \beta_i^{(j)} = f\big(\mathbf{g}_i, \theta^{(j)}\big), \qquad \begin{bmatrix} \beta_1^{(j)} \\ \vdots \\ \beta_N^{(j)} \end{bmatrix} = X\,\theta^{(j)} + \epsilon \quad\text{(Eq. B3),} $$

with a **linear** design (Eq. B4)

$$ X = \begin{bmatrix} g_1^{(x)} & g_1^{(y)} & g_1^{(z)} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ g_N^{(x)} & g_N^{(y)} & g_N^{(z)} \end{bmatrix} $$

or a **quadratic** design that adds the squares and cross-products
$\big(g^{(x)}\big)^2, \big(g^{(y)}\big)^2, \big(g^{(z)}\big)^2, g^{(x)}g^{(y)},
g^{(x)}g^{(z)}, g^{(y)}g^{(z)}$ (Eq. B5). The regularised eddy parameters are the
GLM fit (Eq. B6)

$$ \overline{\beta^{(j)}} = X\,(X^{\mathsf{T}} X)^{-1} X^{\mathsf{T}}\,\beta^{(j)}. $$

This ties eddy distortion to the physics (the field scales with gradient
amplitude/direction), stabilises noisy per-volume estimates, and — as the paper
notes — the fitted $\theta$ can reveal scanner-specific systematic fields.

## Grounding in the project

This is the "volume-wise artifact" nifreeze targets in dMRI. A reimplementation
must decide the polynomial order of the field and whether to regularize across
directions with a second-level GLM; the trade-off (per-volume flexibility vs.
noise) is exactly Eqs. B3–B6. The registration that estimates these parameters
is in [[concept-image-registration]].
